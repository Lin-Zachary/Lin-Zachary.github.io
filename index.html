Studying Japanese Kanji¶

By: Zachary Lin¶

Introduction¶

In the Japanese Language there are three alphabets consisting of hiragana, katakana and kanji. Hiragana and Katakana refer to the number syllabaries used to represent sounds and both consist of 76 commonly used characters. For example, the hiragana character '' makes an 'a' sound and the katakana character '' makes a 'ta' sound. More information about Hiragana and Katakana can be found here. Next comes Kanji which is often one of the most daunting aspects of learning Japanese. Kanji characters are logographic characters that represent readings with each character typically having two or more readings that can be used across many different words. For example, the kanji character '' is used in:
({}{i}ku) meaning 'to go'
({}{okona}u) meaning 'to do'
({}{gyou}retsu) meaning 'line'
({}{kou}dou) meaning 'behavior/conduct'
Among many others.
The specific reading of '' appears in braces. It is estimated that the average Japanese person knows upwards of 4,000 kanji characters.

Buy why even use Kanji? Can we not just replace all the kanji with their readings so that people do not have to learn thousands of characters? However, becaue Japanese does not use spaces between words, kanji is essential for visual clarity and being able to quickly and easily identify the componenets of a sentence. By replacing all kanji with their readings, you can end up with sentences that look like:
(niwaniwaniwaniwatorigairu) (There are two chickens in the garden), which is extremely difficult to read.
However, when using kanji, the sentence becomes:
, which is a lot more comprensible as the individual components can be easily identified and read.
Another important aspect of kanji is that it helps differietiate between homonymns. For example:
(kami) (hair)
(kami) (god)
(kami) (paper)
All three of these words are pronounced the same, but are represented by different kanji characters. (disregarding pitch accent)
More information about kanji can be found here.

As kanji is essential to Japanese, where does one even begin learning it? There are thousands of resources, each with their own kanji lists all presented in different manners and sequences. Some order thier lists by character complexity, some by the grade level and some seemlingly randomly. The aim of this tutorial is to order kanji by frequency as used in a list containing rougly 10,000 commonly used words in Japanese. We will find which kanji are used in the most words and have the most occurances and are thus the most important to learn first. Kanji that appear in less words and have less occurrances, while still important to learn, are less of a priority. Finally, we will attempt to predict comprehension based on how many kanji someone knows, assuming they know all the related vocab the kanji is used in and the grammar of the sentence.

"" also appears in roughly 108 of 8347 words (1.2939%), assuming each occurnace appears in a different word, which is not always the case. See (Sunday) which contains "" twice in one word.
Lets caculate these percents for all 2099 unique kanji in steps of 100. That is, the each step of 100 kanji have X occurances and appear in Y words while accounting for 100/2099 unique characters. We will also do these calculations in a way that does not double count words by simply checking if a word contains at least 1 kanji from the current step.

Now lets do Percent Word Count:
For each step of 100, we will create a temporary list containing those 100 kanji.
We will iterate over the Core10k dataset, counting the number of words that contain at least 1 character from the current step.
The percent word count and total word count will be stored in a dataframe. These percents will add up to over 100% as a word that contains kanji from two different steps will be included in both of those counts. However, words that contain 2 kanji from the same step will not be counted twice.

Hypothesis Testing¶

So what is the point of learning the less frequent kanji that only appear in only a few or sometimes even just one word?
In this section, we will predict comprehension based on how many kanji characters someone knows, assuming knowing the kanji character means you know all the related vocab and grammar. As such, this will be a gross oversimplification of actual comprehension, but will still serve the purpose of showing the importance of learning even the less frequent kanji.

Conclusion¶

The main takeaway from this tutorial is that while all kanji are not equal, some being used more often than others, it is still very important to learn the the vast majority of the commonly used characters. Learning these characters in order of frequency is a solid strategy that will allow learners of Japanese to quickly increase their comprehension.
